Caution!

  • Do NOT touch the 3D printer hotend. It will instantly burn and scar. It operates at 180-230 celsius (370F - 470F).
  • Do NOT touch the 3D printer electronics. You should only touch the electronics if you know what you're doing, or as instructed in the workshop.
  • NEVER move printer parts while the printer is off! Only move them when it's on.
  • Do NOT touch the printer while it's printing! It's better left undisturbed.
  • Avoid breathing fumes from the printer! Melting ABS emits styrene gas and other byproducts. Melted PLA also releases fumes. A 3D printer should be used in a well ventilated area.
  • It's OK to move printer parts by hand while the printer is on.
  • You can learn more about ABS and styrene gas on the internet, for example Wikipedia.
  • Styrene gas is probably or certainly unacceptable for most home uses. Pregnant women and infants should avoid exposure to styrene gas.
  • It's not as clear if PLA byproducts are as risky as styrene gas.

3D Printer Basics

  • 3D Printers are additive manufacturing machines.
  • 3D Printers build objects layer by layer. Objects are printed from bottom up (or top down).
  • 3D Printers can make objects from a variety of materials: plastic, metal, wood, concrete, hybrid materials, biological materials, foodstuffs, and more.
  • 2 classes of 3D printers: deposition and sintering.
  • SSD 3D Printers are filament deposition manufacturing (FDM) machines.
  • SSD 3D Printers use PLA and ABS plastic.
  • Additive manufacturing can create internal structures inside manufactured objects. For example, printed objects can be completely or partially hollow. Indeed, a 3D printer can print a ship inside a bottle.
  • CNC is an example of subtractive manufacturing machine. In subtractive manufacturing, material is discarded (wasted). This process cannot be used to create hollow objects. For example, it cannot be used to manufacture a ship inside a bottle.
  • Deposition and sintering are similar - one process deposits small bits of material side-by-side; the other fuses small bits of material together. The size of the small bits determines the "resolution" of the printed part.

3D Printer Software

  • We use a software toolchain to make things with a 3D printer.
  • CAD software: used to design 3D objects.
  • GCode translator software: convert our CAD model into GCode the 3D printer understands.
  • Control software: control the printer, print GCode files, monitor printer status.
  • What would our world be without CAD software? CAD is probably the most significant software ever made for computers and has literally transformed manufacturing and the human condition.
  • 3D printers (usually) understand the GCode protocol, or a variant of GCode. That's why we convert our CAD files to GCode.
  • CAD: OpenSCAD, FreeCAD, FormIt, Sketchup, OpenJSCAD, TinkerCAD, AutoDesk, AutoCAD, SolidWorks, and more.
  • GCode Translators: Slic3r, Skeinforge, KISSlicer
  • Control: PRINT3R, Octoprint, Pronterface
  • Makerbot has their own software suite, some of it related to and/or based on above products.

Let's Start!

  • Choose a 3D printer for the workshop. Each machine has a label(s) to identify it.
  • Open web browser to http://192.168.1.13:500X (where X is 0, 1, 2, 3, ...)
  • Turn printer ON.
  • Perform pre-flight check.
  • Which port and which X? X corresponds to the label on your 3D printer. For example, use port 5000 for ACM0, port 5001 for ACM1, and so on.

Pre-Flight Check

  • The printer is ON.
  • Identify the control electronics (RAMPS) and motors.
  • Identify axes: X, Y, Z, E.
  • Visually inspect the endstop switches.
  • Verify if filament needs to be loaded.
  • You can move X and Y axes by hand when the printer is on to verify endstop action.
  • Identify the USB cable connected to the printer.

Prepare Heatbed Glass

  • Remove glass from heatbed.
  • Clean glass with soap and water and/or alcohol. Acetone can be used for tough residue, and can be rinsed with isopropyl followed by water.
  • Apply tack to glass. A generous application of hairspray to clean glass is recommended.
  • Re-install glass on heatbed with clips. Ensure the glass is placed flatly on the PCB. Ensure glass clips do not tangle in wires or collide with anything.
  • Re-verify that the z-axis endstop switch is intact.

Connect with Octoprint

  • Web browser opened to http://192.168.1.13:500x.
  • Login using SSD Octoprint credentials. They are the same for every port/instance.
  • Choose the USB port ACMx according to the printer label. ACM0 should choose port 5000, ACM1 chooses port 5001, etc.
  • Baud rate = 115200 (115.2k)
  • Choose the "Console" tab in upper right.
  • Click the Connect button near upper left. Printer state = "Connecting".
  • Observe the printer response in the console. It should be human readable and indicate the printer is ready. Octoprint's printer state will change to "Connected".
  • If Octoprint behaves erratically, try refreshing the page. Example below.
  • If the printer does not connect then Octoprint may become confused. If you click connect and the connect button is still disabled after ~10 seconds, then refresh the web page.
  • If the printer does not connect then try these things:
    • Ask the instructor how to reset your RAMPS.
    • Check the USB cable. Try re-seating it especially on the printer side even if it looks OK. The Arduino LEDs should flash on the printer when the USB cable is re-connected.
    • Try connecting with Octoprint again.
    • Change the baud rate to 230400 in Octoprint, then click Connect. Wait for this to fail (as it should), if Octoprint behaves erratically then refresh. Change the baud rate back to 115200, then click Connect. Wait for this to succeed. If it still doesn't connect, repeat the process; in the worst case let somebody know and use another printer.

Caution Before Jogging

  • Moving a print axis is also called "jogging" it.
  • A "crash" occurs when an axis goes beyond a minimum or maximum.
  • If X or Y axes crash the stepper motor(s) makes a nasty noise. It's not good for the motor but the machine will survive.
  • If Z axis crashes then something might break. The hotend makes contact with and pushes down on the (semi-rigid) heatbed. The hotend can suffer damage and the heatbed glass can shatter. The heatbed has springs in order to absorb some of this, but how much varies on each machine (sometimes they are already compressed). If the z-axis keeps pushing then the glass will shatter and the metal parts and frame will bend.
  • If the z-axis crashes then we can (usually) adjust the endstop position. We must also re-check the bed-levelling.
  • ALWAYS click "Motors Off" in the Control tab in Octoprint if you want to move any X/Y/Z axis by hand.
  • SSD printers have "min endstops", so if a crash happens on the minimum side then something is wrong with the endstop switch. Crashes on the minimum side are more frequent because SSD printers "home" at "zero", which means they start prints from the minimum of each axis.

Warm Up Printer

  • Choose the Control tab in Octoprint.
  • Click the X/Y Home icon.
  • Did you check the Z-endstop switch?
  • Click the Z Home icon.
  • Choose the Temperature tab.
  • Set the Bed Temperature to PLA (60C).
  • Set the Temperature (hotend) to 0 (off).
  • ALWAYS click "Motors Off" in the Control tab in Octoprint if you want to move any X/Y/Z axis by hand.

Generate SSD Emblem GCode

  • Click/drag the SSDEmblem.stl file into the Slic3r "plater" (the main screen).
  • Alternatively, click "Add" on the main screen of Slic3r. Then browse to the SSDEmbled.stl file.
  • Click Export GCode. Save the GCode file in your work folder.
  • Use a verbose file name. A recommended format is [name_of_object]-[printer_model]-[material_type].gcode. For example, ssd-emblem-twmm16-pla.gcode.
  • Familiarize yourself with basic view and edit actions in the Slic3r plater.

Load SSD Emblem GCode

  • In OctoPrint, scroll to the bottom-left area.
  • Click "Upload GCode".
  • Browse to your work folder. Choose the SDDEmblem GCode file you saved earlier and click OK.
  • Octoprint will show the upload progress. Once finished, the uploaded GCode file will appear in the Octoprint file list.
  • Find the uploaded file in the Octoprint file list. Cilck the folder icon next to the filename to load it.
  • We can view the estimated file time and filament length once Octoprint has analyzed the GCode. This happens after the file is uploaded.

Verify GCode in Viewer

  • Click the GCode Viewer tab in Octoprint.
  • Familiarize yourself with the horizontal and vertical scrollbars in the view area.
  • Verify that the first layer is what you expect.
  • The GCode Viewer has several options listed beneath the viewer.

Ship It!

  • Click Print in Octoprint. The startup process is contained the first several lines of GCode and is described below.
  • The printer starts by heating the heatbed. If it's already warm, this step completes quickly.
  • Next, the printer moves X/Y/Z axes to zero/home. For practical purposes, this is the start of the print (see note).
  • Next, the printer heats the hotend. When filament ooze appears the printer should be near target temperature.
  • Lastly, the printer starts the first layer of the object. It executes GCode line-by-line, many lines per second so that its motions appear smooth.
  • If something goes wrong then perform a hard reset: turn the machine off (power button) - then reset in Octoprint by clicking Cancel, then Disconnect, then Connect.
  • If your pre-flight check missed something, or something happened afterwards, then this is an unfortunate time to learn, and timing gets worse afterwards.
  • The recommended reset process above is called a "hard reset". There is also a "soft reset" process that can be used if the printer has already started the first layer: click "Cancel" in Octoprint, then jog the Z-axis upwards 10mm.
  • In any case, a soft reset must wait for the printer to clear its motion buffer, which usually ranges from 4-16 commands (moves).
  • Another reset process uses the RAMPS reset button but this requires reaching into or near the frame while it operates.

Get Yer Parts

  • Wait for the heatbed to cool to room or handling temperature.
  • The printed plastic part cures as it cools - a fascinating process.
  • After enough cooling parts will usually peel themselves from the glass (by contraction) or can be removed by hand.
  • If the part is stuck, or you cannot wait, see notes.
  • Disconnect from the printer and turn it off.
  • (optional) Carefully remove the heatbed glass to expedite the cooling process. Be careful with glass!
  • (optional) Place the glass on a flat surface to expedite cooling further.
  • (optional) If the part is not removable by hand then carefully remove the glass; next carefully use a sharp-edge knife or blade to lift corners of the part away from the glass; create enough space to leverage the part from the glass; and finally lift the part from the glass.
